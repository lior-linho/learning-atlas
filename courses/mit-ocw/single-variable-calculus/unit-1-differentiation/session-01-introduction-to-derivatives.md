## Goal

Understand the derivative as the slope of a tangent line and as the limit of secant slopes.

## Core Ideas

- A secant line measures average change between two points.
- A tangent line describes local behavior near one point.
- The derivative is the limiting slope of secant lines as the second point approaches the first point.
- The derivative gives the instantaneous rate of change of a function.

## Main Formula

For a function \( f(x) \), the derivative at \( x \) is:

\[
f'(x) = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{f(x+\Delta x)-f(x)}{\Delta x}
\]

Equivalent notation:

\[
f'(a) = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a}
\]

## My Understanding

The key idea is that differentiation turns a changing quantity into a local rate of change.

Instead of asking how much a function changes over a visible interval, the derivative asks what the function is doing at one point.

## Mistakes to Watch

- Confusing average rate of change with instantaneous rate of change.
- Treating the tangent line as just a visual object rather than a limiting process.
- Forgetting that the derivative depends on a point unless a full derivative function is being computed.
